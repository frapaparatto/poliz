## ADR-001: Primary Key Strategy

**Date:** March 2026
**Status:** DECIDED

### Context

Every entity in the CRM (Client, Policy, Interaction) needs a stable
internal identity so repositories can find, update, and delete records
unambiguously. The initial question was whether to use the client's
email address as the primary key or to generate a separate surrogate
key.

Email is already required and unique per client, which makes it a
natural candidate for an identity field. However, email is also a
mutable attribute: a client can change their email address, and that
change would need to propagate to every policy and interaction that
references them.

### Options Considered

**Option A: Email as primary key**

Use the email address directly as the unique identifier for clients.
Store email in policies and interactions as the foreign reference.
No UUID generation required.

The problem: email is user-controlled and mutable. If a client changes
their email, every policy row and every interaction row holding that
email as a foreign key must be updated atomically. The update either
needs a cascade mechanism or must be done manually. Any inconsistency
leaves orphaned records with a stale email that can no longer be
resolved to the modified client.

Beyond mutability, email as PK leaks internal identity to every layer.
Policy and interaction records store a human-readable field as their
foreign key, which ties the data model to the presentation layer.

**Option B: UUID v4 as surrogate key, email as unique constraint**

Assign each entity a randomly generated UUID at creation time. Store
the UUID as the internal identity field used in all foreign references.
Enforce email uniqueness separately in the service layer. The UUID
never changes; email can change freely without touching any related
record.

### Decision

**Option B.** UUID v4 as surrogate key, email as user-facing unique
constraint enforced by the service layer.

### Rationale

The UUID is stable for the lifetime of the entity. A client changing
their email is a routine operation that requires updating one field on
one record. No cascade, no broken references, no orphaned policies.

Email uniqueness is enforced by ClientService::addClient and
ClientService::editClient via findByEmail before committing any change.
The check lives in the service layer because it is a business rule
(no two clients share an email), not a data structure invariant. The
repository stores what it receives; the service guards the constraint.

UUID generation uses a manual v4 implementation in utils.cpp based on
mt19937 seeded from std::random_device. No external library was added.
The implementation follows RFC 4122 variant 1 version 4, produces the
standard 8-4-4-4-12 hyphenated hex format, and is sufficient for a
single-process CRM with no global uniqueness requirement. Thread safety
was added later via a static mutex when AutoSave introduced a second
thread (see ADR-024).

The UUID is never displayed to the user. The CLI uses email for
identity in searches and the resolution pipeline. The UUID is an
internal key only, visible in the CSV files but never surfaced in
the interface.

### Impact

- Client has two constructors: one for creation (generates UUID via
generateUuid inside the constructor body), one for loading from CSV
(accepts the UUID as a parameter). The same pattern is applied to
Policy and Interaction.
- UUID generation happens inside Client::Client(...), Policy::Policy(...),
and the Interaction/Appointment/Contract new-object constructors, not
in the service layer. CsvClientRepository::deserialize reads the stored
UUID from the CSV row and passes it to the load constructor.
- Email uniqueness is checked in ClientService::addClient and in
ClientService::editClient (when the email field changes). The check
calls findByEmail and throws std::invalid_argument if a match is found.
